From the African rhythms of colonial Cuba to the streets of New York, Cali, and Havana — a guide to every style, its origins, its artists, and how to hear the difference

What we call salsa is not a single style — it is a living family of genres, each shaped by a different city, era, and cultural moment. "Timba," "salsa dura," "romántica," "Colombian salsa" — these are not just labels, they are doorways into distinct musical worlds.

Where It All Begins
The Roots:
Son Cubano & the Clave

Before the branches, there is the trunk. All salsa — every style, every city, every era — descends from son cubano, a genre born in eastern Cuba in the 1860s and matured in Havana by the 1920s. Son blended Spanish guitar with African percussion and call-and-response singing, and it introduced the one element that unifies every form of salsa to this day: the clave.

The clave is a five-note rhythmic pattern — either 3-2 or 2-3 — that functions as the genetic code of the music. Every instrument, every phrase, every dance step ultimately relates back to it. Learn to hear the clave and every song reveals its underlying architecture.

The figure who transformed son into something closer to modern salsa was Arsenio Rodríguez, a blind Cuban tres player in the 1940s who added a conga drum, a second trumpet, and a piano to the traditional son group. He also developed the extended instrumental section called the montuno — the repeated, improvisatory vamp over which singers and instrumentalists solo. This is the part of a salsa song where you let loose on the dance floor.

Style 01
Salsa Dura
New York City · Puerto Rico · 1960s–1980s
Hard Salsa

When the U.S. trade embargo cut off direct exchange with Cuba in 1962, Puerto Rican and Cuban musicians living in New York fused the Cuban tradition with jazz, rhythm and blues, and the raw energy of the barrio. The Fania record label branded the result "salsa" from 1964 onward, functioning essentially as the Motown of Latin music.

Salsa dura is characterized by aggressive trombone-heavy horn arrangements, driving percussion, complex clave interplay, and lyrics that were often socially conscious, political, or street-level storytelling. The tempo is firm and purposeful — it drives rather than flows. Vocalists improvise in extended soneo sections over repeating horn and rhythm vamps.

This is music that carries weight. It documented immigrant life in New York, celebrated Afro-Caribbean identity, and mourned the struggles of the barrio. When you dance to salsa dura, you feel the gravity of that story.

Style 02
Salsa Romántica
Puerto Rico · New York · 1985–present
Romantic / Sensual

By the mid-1980s, salsa dura had begun to feel heavy for commercial radio. A new style emerged that prioritized love songs, smoother arrangements, and more accessible productions. Salsa romántica defined what most people heard on Latin radio from about 1985 through the 1990s.

The tempos are generally slower, the horn sections polished to a radio-friendly sheen, and the lyrics focus on romance, heartbreak, and longing. Eddie Santiago is credited with launching the style with his 1986 album Atrevido y Diferente. Frankie Ruiz became its most beloved star with hits like "La Cura" before his untimely death in 1998.

Purists sometimes dismiss salsa romántica as watered-down, but for dancers it has real value. The slower tempos and cleaner structures make it wonderful for partner connection — many social dance events include a lot of it precisely because it works so well for close partner dancing.

Style 03
Colombian Salsa
Cali, Colombia · 1970s–present
Cali Style

While New York developed salsa dura and Puerto Rico refined salsa romántica, something remarkable was happening in the Colombian Pacific coastal city of Cali, which eventually earned the nickname "the world capital of salsa." The Colombian scene absorbed and transformed the New York sound into something distinctly its own.

Colombian salsa is notably faster, with a rhythmic feel that emphasizes upbeat energy. It blends salsa with elements of Colombian cumbia, Caribbean rhythms, and its own coastal musical traditions. Joe Arroyo, from Cartagena, created his own style he called "joeson" — his landmark song "La Rebelión" tells the story of an enslaved African couple in colonial Cartagena and is one of the most beloved songs in all of Latin music.

Caleño dancers developed incredibly rapid, intricate footwork to match the music's pace — if you have ever watched Colombian salsa dancing, you understand immediately that this style demands a different physical vocabulary than New York or Cuban salsa.

Style 04
Timba
Havana, Cuba · Late 1980s–present
Cuban Funk Salsa

Timba is what happened when Cuban musicians — cut off from the commercial salsa industry developing in New York and Puerto Rico — took their own radical path. To understand timba, you must understand the context: Cuba entered the Special Period in 1990 after the Soviet collapse, a time of severe economic hardship. Out of crisis came explosive creativity.

Bands mixed son and Afro-Cuban rumba traditions with American funk, jazz fusion, and hip-hop, producing a sound unlike anything else. Timba is denser, more percussive, and more compositionally complex than New York salsa. Its bass lines are aggressive and highly syncopated. Its piano tumbaos are jagged and funk-influenced — chord stabs that hit in unexpected places. Its horn arrangements explode in short bursts rather than smooth melodic lines.

Crucially, timba songs contain bloques — structured percussion breakdowns where the rhythm section drops into a groove and the horns hit in rhythmic punches. These breakdowns are not random: they are compositional cues that tell the dancer exactly when to do specific figures. Casino dancing evolved in direct dialogue with timba, which is why understanding this style is essential for any serious salsa dancer.

The founding figure is José Luis Cortés of NG La Banda, who essentially invented the genre in 1989. But the band you most need to know is Los Van Van, founded in 1969 by bassist Juan Formell, who spent decades evolving Cuban popular music through a transitional style called songo before timba crystallized around them.

For the Dancer
How to Tell the
Styles Apart

Listen to the Bass

Timba bass is aggressive, syncopated, almost dancing on its own. Classic salsa bass (tumbao) feels steadier and more anticipatory. Colombian salsa bass pushes relentlessly forward.

Listen to the Piano

Timba piano is jagged and funk-influenced — chord stabs that syncopate heavily. Classic salsa piano flows with arpeggiated montuno patterns. Romántica piano is melodic and smooth.

Listen to the Horns

Salsa dura uses trombones heavily with long melodic phrases. Timba horns hit in short explosive bursts called bloques. Colombian salsa uses bright, brassy trumpet-heavy arrangements.

Listen for Breakdowns

If the band suddenly drops into a drum-and-bass groove with the singer chanting short phrases over it, you are hearing timba. These bloques are your cue to do specific casino figures.
