# Salsa song structure breakdowns: source analysis compilation

**Bottom line:** Comprehensive, published timestamped breakdowns for salsa are rare — only about **6 songs** have confirmed fully-timestamped section maps available online, with another **10–12 songs** having partial timestamps or ordered structural prose. The gold-standard sources are Salsa Forums (user "Curtis/Groovetpt"), Che's Music Blog on Salsayo, Son y Casino, The Dance Dojo, Rueda Con Ritmo, and LatinScores arranger notes. For the 5-style ask, Salsa Dura and Timba are well-served; Salsa Romántica has 1–2 fully-mapped examples; Salsa Colombiana has **no** publicly-timestamped breakdowns and will require manual annotation (MSAF or SongFormer tools are the best automated starting point). A salsa-specific MIR beat dataset (124 expert-annotated tracks) does exist and can supply the rhythmic layer.

---

## The canonical salsa structure formula

Before diving into individual songs, every source converges on this reference template for classic NY/PR salsa dura:

**Intro → Cuerpo/Tema (Verse) → [Puente/Bridge] → Montuno I (Coro-Pregón) → Mambo → Montuno II → Moña → Montuno III → Coda**

Key rules documented by Salsa Forums analysts, Wikipedia's "Salsa (musical structure)" article, and Don Baarns' Music4Dancers channel:

- **Section lengths follow the "Probably Four" principle** — most sections last 4 or 8 clave cycles (8 or 16 bars of 4/4). Counting "1-2-3-4, 2-2-3-4, 3-2-3-4, 4-2-3-4" will usually land on a section change.
- **Mambo vs. Moña (NY/PR usage):** There is typically only **one mambo** (the first elaborate instrumental horn section after the first montuno, often modulating to 3-2 clave); there can be **multiple moñas** (shorter horn riffs that stay on the montuno chord progression). A mambo is "too complicated" to be a moña — it deviates from the montuno chord progression and includes breaks.
- **Cuban usage inverts the terms:** in Cuba, each horn line itself is called a "mambo" (what NY calls a moña); the vamp section is simply "montuno."
- **Clave:** ~80% of classic NY/PR salsa (1955–1985) is **2-3 son clave** with no direction change; ~1 in 20 features a 2-3 → 3-2 shift, usually at the mambo section.
- **Instrumentation cues mark sections:** Theme = bongocero plays *martillo*, timbalero plays *cáscara*. Montuno = bongocero switches to **campana (cowbell)**, timbalero moves to **mambo bell** and "cáscara mambo."

Timba adds to this vocabulary: **Guías** (vocal improvisations bridging coros, or rhythmic "gears"), **Bomba/Presión** (climactic high-energy gear — aggressive conga/drums, loud bass slides, cued by "¡Bomba!"), **Marcha** (basic gear), **Pedal** (stripped-down gear — no conga, sporadic bass pedal tones), **Bloque** (unison percussion+bass hit), **Despelote** (frenzied dance response during bomba).

## Timba / Cuban Salsa

### Ahora, Que Buscas? — Havana D'Primera ⭐⭐⭐ THE GOLD STANDARD

**Metadata:** Alexander Abreu y Havana D'Primera, album *Haciendo Historia* (2009). Modern son / timba. **Source:** Che's Music Blog on Salsayo — https://salsayo.com/blogs/cuban_music_che/?p=178 (author: Michael Che Morozov, Oct 11, 2013).

**This is the single most detailed published salsa breakdown found** — reproduced largely verbatim below because the user specifically requested it.

**00:00 – 00:16 — Intro.** 12 bars (unusual — not 8 or 16). Horns play melody, percussion plays typical patterns, atmosphere set, everything suave and calm, no singing.

**00:17 – 02:02 — Cuerpo.**
- 00:17 — Alexander starts verse 1; piano supports with melody/chords; horns drop in occasionally; percussionists play typical patterns.
- 00:38 — After 16 bars, **1st Rumba-flavored break** (~8 bars, slight horn breaks on bars 6, 7, 8).
- 00:59 — **Mambo/Son Montuno-influenced break** with campana (8 bars).
- 01:08 — Return to "regular" cuerpo for 8 bars.
- 01:21 — Horns take charge for 8 bars with small breaks on bars 5, 6, 7, 8.
- 01:30 — Vocals return for 16 bars; slight horn break on bar 8 and bar 1 after.
- 01:51 — Another 8 bars of cuerpo.
- 02:02 — **Timbalero plays short break, bongocero switches to campana** → Montuno begins.

**02:02 – ~05:00 — Montuno.**
- 02:14 — **Coro-Pregón** begins (back-vocals answer Alexander).
- 02:25 — Short horn & percussion break, then coro-pregón continues at full swing.
- 02:44 — Series of small breaks led by horns with percussion, at 02:45, 02:46, 02:51–02:56, 03:03–03:07.
- 03:07 – 03:16 — **Rumba break**, call-and-response between Alexander and back-vocals.
- 03:38 — **Small Mambo (Moña) section** — horns mingle with vocals and percussion.
- 03:51 — Slight **percussion solo**.
- 04:21 – 04:41 — Another **Rumba-flavored break / guía**, then return to regular montuno.
- ~05:00 — Alexander sings "se acabó lo que tu esperaba"; volume drops; slow fade-out (no defined ending).

**Instrumentation cues:** Cuerpo = conguero plays 1-bar tumbao, bass plays bass tumbao, bongocero plays martillo, timbalero plays basic cáscara (no bells), piano plays melody/supporting chords. Montuno = bongocero switches to campana, timbalero plays "cáscara mambo" + mambo bell.

### Para bailar casino — Adalberto Álvarez y Su Son ⭐

**Metadata:** Adalberto Álvarez y Su Son, son montuno / salsa cubana. **Source:** Son y Casino blog, Daybert Linares Tamayo, April 20, 2017 — https://sonycasino.com/2017/04/20/musicality-let-the-mambo-section-guide-when-you-do-your-paseos/

| Time | Section | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 0:00 – 0:21 | Intro (wind/brass-dominant) | "Predominant instruments are wind and brass for a good 21 seconds at the beginning" |
| 0:21 – ~1:09 | Cuerpo / Largo / Canto | Singer tells the story; calmer; dancers stay closed-position, do paseos |
| ~1:09 – 1:28 | Montuno begins | Instrumentation picks up aggressive feel; coro-pregón estribillo begins: Singer "¿Dónde está María? ¿Dónde está Fernando?" / Chorus "Bailando, mamá, bailando casino" |
| 1:28 – onwards | **Mambo section** (cued by shout) | "You can hear another explicit reference to the mambo section here at 1:28" — wind/brass take center stage, singer shouts "¡mambo!" |

**Vocabulary introduced:** *Largo/Canto* = Cuerpo (narrative). *Guías* = lead-vocal improvised lines between coro repetitions (rooted in live rumba). *Mambo cue* = singer's shout of "¡mambo!" or Alexander Abreu's catchphrase "¡Camina por arriba del mambo!" Dance application: closed position + paseos for cuerpo; open position + turn patterns for montuno; switch to paseos during mambo to "recharge."

### La Tremenda — Bamboleo ⭐ FULL FORM CHART

**Metadata:** Bamboleo (director Lázaro Valdés), album *Ya No Hace Falta* (2001). 132 total measures. **Source:** Rueda Con Ritmo — https://www.ruedaconritmo.com/media (downloadable Form Chart).

**Sections (in order, labeled in the chart):**
- **Intro** — short, pre-cuerpo (~pre-0:17)
- **CUERPO** (~0:17 onward) — single lead singer; harmonic/melodic/lyrical variety; percussionists play softly (timbalero on drum sides, not bells); dancers closed position
- **Bridge/Puente** — short transition
- **MONTUNO** — call-and-response. Features three distinct **CORO 1, CORO 2, CORO 3** (CORO 1 has a shortened "picao" variant labeled CORO 1b, repeating at measures 41, 49, 57, etc.). Lead sings **GUÍAS** between coros. Piano/bass/keys lock into repeating tumbaos.
- **3 MAMBOS** (horn-driven riffs, numbered in chart)
- **BLOQUES** (unison percussion+bass hits — characteristic "Bamboleo Bloque")
- **GEARS** (italicized in chart): *Marcha* (basic — full percussion + bass, timbalero on bells, campana full force) / *Pedal* (conga drops out, sparse bass pedal tones) / *Bomba* (aggressive conga/drums, loud bass slides — climactic)
- **Coda** (optional)

**Teaching value:** Definitive example of timba's **gear system** — moments where the whole band shifts rhythmic mode on cue ("¡Bomba!"), which is the single feature most distinguishing timba from 1970s/80s salsa.

### Additional Havana D'Primera qualitative notes (timba.com, Kevin Moore)

Live Havana D'Primera arrangements typically follow: **guaguancó intro → soft cuerpo → rumba percussion break → first coro (duro) → bomba (often with trumpet solo) → muele (Abreu speaks to crowd) → new coro**. For "Mi Música" (Ian Stewart Bass analysis, https://www.ianstewartbass.com/blog/2018/2/26/), the montuno section begins at **2:00**, chord progression is 4 bars / 2 claves in length, **2-3 clave direction**. The general rule: "Whenever the bass is playing a tumbao, the band is in *marcha*; when the bass is doing anything else, the band is in some form of *gear cycle*."

## Salsa Colombiana

**Critical finding:** After thorough searching in English and Spanish, **no publicly-indexed timestamped section-by-section breakdowns exist for the canonical Colombian salsa tracks** (Cali Pachanguero, Una Aventura, Sin Sentimiento, La Rebelión, Tania, El Preso, Oiga Mire Vea). The webpage will need to generate these manually or via automated tools. What exists is structural *order* from chord charts, arranger interviews, and one academic thesis.

## AI tools and datasets for automated analysis

Because publicly-timestamped salsa breakdowns are scarce, automated tools are the most scalable path. Nine resources are viable; three deserve immediate evaluation:

### Salsa Beat Estimation Dataset (Gómez-Marín et al., 2024) ⭐ SALSA-SPECIFIC
- **Paper:** https://transactions.ismir.net/articles/10.5334/tismir.183
- **Data:** https://zenodo.org/records/13120822
- **Contents:** 124 expert-annotated salsa tracks with beat annotations, spectrograms, audio fragments. Explicitly addresses clave-centric beat ambiguity.
- **Verdict:** ★★★★★ for rhythm/beat layer. Provides beats but **not section labels**. Only salsa-specific MIR dataset known to exist. Contact authors for full audio access (academic use).

### MSAF — Music Structure Analysis Framework ⭐ BEST TOOL
- **Repo:** https://github.com/urinieto/msaf-data (and associated MSAF Python package by Oriol Nieto)
- **Contents:** Python framework with multiple segmentation algorithms (Foote, C-NMF, Structural Features, Spectral Clustering, OLDA). Loaders for SALAMI, Isophonics, Beatles.
- **Verdict:** ★★★★★ — The best candidate to run automatic structural segmentation on any salsa MP3 and produce candidate timestamped section boundaries. Audio-agnostic; no pretrained salsa model but algorithms generalize.

### SongFormer / SongFormDB (2025, SOTA transformer)
- **Dataset:** https://huggingface.co/datasets/ASLP-lab/SongFormDB
- **Contents:** Large multilingual MSA dataset, pretrained transformer (HR.5F=0.703, ACC=0.807 on HarmonixSet).
- **Verdict:** ★★★★☆ — Pretrained model you can run on salsa audio directly. Latin/salsa not explicitly in training data but likely generalizes reasonably well.

## Conclusions and recommendations

**The four songs ready to publish immediately** with full timestamped structure are **Esa Prieta** (salsa dura template), **Ahora Que Buscas?** (timba template, with bar-level micro-breakdown), **Nadie Como Ella** (salsa romántica template), and **La Tremenda** (timba gear-system template). Add **Conciencia**, **Y Que Me Pasa**, and **De Patitas** for a strong starter set of 7 songs covering three of the five styles with gold-standard sourcing.

**For the remaining styles**, the webpage must generate annotations manually. The recommended workflow: run **MSAF** or **SongFormer** on the audio to produce candidate segment boundaries, then hand-correct using the canonical salsa-dura formula and the Rueda Con Ritmo form-chart conventions. Validate rhythmic/beat alignment against the **Gómez-Marín 2024 Salsa Beat Dataset** if the track is among the 124.

**The biggest gap is Salsa Colombiana** — zero timestamped breakdowns exist publicly. Building even one authoritative timestamped annotation of "Cali Pachanguero" or "La Rebelión" would be a genuine contribution to the online salsa-musicality literature. The Universidad El Bosque thesis on Joe Arroyo (dedicated form-analysis sections 12.3.1–12.3.3) is the closest existing scholarship and should be obtained.

**Critical vocabulary distinction** to bake into webpage UX: the **mambo/moña** terminology is inverted between Cuban and NY/PR traditions. The webpage should either pick a convention and stick to it, or expose a toggle — otherwise users from different scenes will read the same section labels and disagree about what they mean.

**Avoid the Spotify Audio Analysis API** as a primary source — it has been independently documented as unreliable for salsa, which lacks the verse/chorus contrast its algorithms depend on, and it frequently reports salsa at half-tempo.
